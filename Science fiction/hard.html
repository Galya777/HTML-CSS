Hard science fiction

Hard science fiction is characterized by an emphasis on scientific accuracy. The term was first used in print in 1957 by P. Schuyler Miller in a review of John W. Campbell's Islands of Space in Astounding Science Fiction.[24][25][26] The complementary term soft science fiction, formed by analogy to hard science fiction,[27] first appeared in the late 1970s; referring to the popular distinction between the "hard" (natural) and "soft" (social) sciences. Science fiction critic Gary Westfahl argues that neither term is part of a rigorous taxonomy; instead they are approximate ways of characterizing stories that reviewers and commentators have found useful.

Some hard SF authors have distinguished themselves as working scientists, including Gregory Benford, Fred Hoyle, Geoffrey A. Landis, David Brin,[29] and Robert L. Forward, while mathematician authors include Rudy Rucker and Vernor Vinge. Other noteworthy hard SF authors include Isaac Asimov, Arthur C. Clarke, Hal Clement, Greg Bear, Larry Niven, Catherine Asaro, Robert J. Sawyer, Stephen Baxter, Nancy Kress, Alastair Reynolds, Charles Sheffield, C. J. Cherryh, Ben Bova, Kim Stanley Robinson, Andy Weir and Greg Egan.
